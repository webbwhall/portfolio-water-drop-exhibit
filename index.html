Project Overview

This project is an immersive museum exhibit concept where visitors follow one drop of water from rainfall in Cincinnati skies, through streams and soil, into the Ohio River, and eventually into ecosystems, human life, treatment systems, and industry. The goal is to make the water cycle feel like a story instead of just a science diagram.

Big Idea

The exhibit frames water as a character with a journey. Visitors move through zones of the water cycle: Precipitation Station, Collection City, Evaporation Enclave, and Condensation Country. Each zone teaches a different part of the water cycle through physical installations, projection mapping, touch screens, app interactions, and guided storytelling.

Rainfall → Streams & Soil → Ohio River → Human Life & Ecosystems → Evaporation → Clouds

Final Direction

Direction A locks a deep aquatic palette, luminous cyan accents, and high-contrast typography so environmental graphics read at distance while digital surfaces stay approachable for families on phones and touch stations.

Design priorities

  • Immersive science content that still invites touch, tap, and participation
  • Shared visual language across signage, floor screens, web, and companion app
  • Ohio River narrative specificity instead of generic stock nature
  • Clear spatial sequencing through exhibit zones and supporting digital flows
  • Legible type and controls under mixed gallery lighting

Typography & Branding Exploration

I tested different headline styles to find a balance between playful, educational, and readable display type for large environmental graphics and digital screens.

The chosen direction favors rounded forms and high-contrast outlines so headlines stay legible across hero imagery, signage, and app screens without feeling childish or overly ornamental.

AI Exploration Journey

Generative tools helped visualize atmosphere fast, then narrowed toward the Ohio River story.

The early AI results were too literal and looked like textbook diagrams. Through more specific prompts, the visuals became more immersive and focused on the Ohio River story. AI became a starting point, not the final design by itself.

Usability Testing & Improvements

Validation focused on wayfinding cues, ticketing clarity, and perceived interactivity versus static content.

Participants generally understood ticketing steps, though some were unsure where they could safely tap or probe without breaking narrative flow. Iterations prioritized explicit affordances, bolder hierarchy for exploratory modules, clearer selected controls in scheduling, and tightened feedback within learning sequences.

Problem

Visitors missed subtle interactive cues on complex panels.

Change

Introduced bolder prompts, segmented buttons, and motion hints where appropriate.

Problem

Explore / Stations visuals blended into informational sections.

Change

Increased typographic hierarchy, breathing room, and contrast blocks for scan-friendly discovery.

Problem

Ticket time selection states felt tentative.

Change

Strengthened active + hover treatments and paired labels with confirmations.

Moodboards & Visual Direction

Early explorations anchored the exhibit in immersive blues, luminous highlights, and family-friendly clarity.

Direction A felt more energetic, playful, immersive, and family-friendly, so it became the stronger direction for the exhibit. Direction B was elegant and calm, but felt less engaging for younger visitors.

Reflection

This project helped me understand how a design system can connect physical space, digital interaction, storytelling, and education. I carried a broad immersive idea through exhibit identity decisions including branding, UX journeys, annotated wireframes, and app concepts supporting on-floor scans, signage, and hero illustrations, while iterating with real usability feedback.

The biggest challenge was making the water cycle feel immersive rather than schematic. The breakthrough came from narrowing the story to one droplet traversing Cincinnati rainfall into the Ohio River, then layering human and ecological intersections on that arc.
