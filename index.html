You can scroll straight through or bounce with the nav. Programs and Wireframes are the nerdy parts. Live site and Figma are under Website and App when you are ready to click stuff.

Exhibit-wise it is a fake museum walkthrough. One drop, Cincinnati rain, creeks, mud, Ohio River, then plants and fish and pipes and drinking water, then evaporation again. Classic cycle but tied to here.

I really did not want it to feel like a fifth grade poster with arrows in a circle. More like you are actually walking a line.

Role

Concept, UX/UI, visuals, branding, all me for the project.

Audience

Families, school buses, random Tuesday visitors, people who are not trying to read a spreadsheet on a wall.

Goal

Make the science feel like Ohio, not like a textbook chapter that could be anywhere.

Format

Promo website, phone app, plus signs I mocked into hallway photos.

Why it matters

Lots of people grow up next to the Ohio River and still do not think about where their water actually comes from or where it goes after.

So I kept dragging it back to Ohio stuff. If it felt like it could be any river on any continent, I was doing it wrong.

Big idea

The drop is basically the main character. I gave the zones goofy names on purpose so it feels less like a lecture: Precipitation Station, Collection City, Evaporation Enclave, Condensation Country. Yes I know it is cheesy, it worked in my head.

Each stop is supposed to be physical stuff plus a screen or two plus the app nudging you, so you are not just staring at a paragraph and leaving.

Rainfall → Streams & Soil → Ohio River → Human Life & Ecosystems → Evaporation → Clouds

Final direction

Dark blue, glowy cyan, lots of contrast. Gallery lighting is always bad so I was designing for worst case. Also I did not want a family standing there like, cool, where do I even tap.

Stuff I kept asking myself

  • Does this feel hands-on or just wall text
  • Can a parent and a kid actually use it at the same time
  • Do the signs match the app or does it look like two different museums
  • Does it read Ohio or generic nature stock
  • Could I walk it without getting spun around

App on the floor

In the building the phone is mostly map, next step, scan codes, see what you already did. If that works the rooms feel linked. If it does not, you are just in random rooms with pretty walls.

How I picture someone actually using it

  1. 1
    Walk in

    Open app, glance at map, figure out where to start walking.

  2. 2
    Scan the marker

    Camera on the code so the right station loads and you are not in the wrong room pretending.

  3. 3
    Do the station

    Short prompts, little reactions, so it feels like doing a thing instead of reading a novel on a wall.

  4. 4
    Save progress

    Collect stations or badges so you feel the line moving.

  5. 5
    Finish the arc

    Last screen ties evaporation and everyday water back in so the loop feels finished.

AR scanning

Scan starts a little guided moment for that one stop.

Collecting stations

Save zones you find so you have a reason to finish the whole path instead of bailing halfway.

Finishing the cycle

Ending screen brings wildlife, faucets, evaporation back together so it feels like one story, not random facts.

Learning between taps

Little prompts and feedback so families learn something without standing in a doorway forever.

Honestly on the floor I only care about two things: where am I on this map, what do I do next. Everything else is extra. Get those right and it feels like one path, not five random rooms.

Testing and fixes

I grabbed friends and a couple classmates, had them buy a ticket and click around the station pages, mostly to see what they thought was a button.

Ticketing was fine. The thing that broke people was dense panels where nothing looked tappable. After that I cranked buttons, spacing, and selected states way up.

What I actually did

  • Talk out loud while they bought tickets and poked station pages.
  • First click on the messy sections to see if buttons even read as buttons.
  • Notes right after each session, then the longer write-up pages under here.

Problem

People missed small interactive cues on packed panels.

Change

Bigger prompts, chunkier buttons, tiny motion only where it actually helped.

Problem

Explore and Stations sections visually blended into the text blocks around them.

Change

More air between chunks, bolder type, and darker panels behind the interactive rows so skimming is faster.

Problem

Ticket time slots did not look locked in when you picked them.

Change

Stronger selected and hover states, and labels that confirm what you just picked.

This is where I stopped doomscrolling Pinterest and actually picked a vibe. Still a museum, still kid friendly, still readable when you are half asleep on a bus ride here.

Random notes

  • References: map apps with obvious progress, museum signs with huge type, posters that use real contrast instead of gray on gray.
  • Two directions: A and B lived side by side for a bit. A was louder and more kid without turning into chaos on a wall.
  • Cutting stuff: if I was tired and could not read it fast, it did not make the cut. Also had to still feel like the same river story all the way through.

Direction A won for me because it felt more alive for families and matched the energy I wanted on the floor.

B still looks nice on a PDF honestly, but it did not drag little kids through the space the same way in my head.

Reflection

Biggest lesson: keeping a fake walkthrough and a phone app feeling like the same show is annoying work. Brand, web, app, signs all have to share one visual language or it looks like four different projects.

Hardest part was the water cycle because it is so easy to make it feel like fifth grade homework. Once I stuck to one drop in Ohio and tied it to pipes, plants, faucets, it finally clicked for me.

If I did it again I would test ticketing way earlier with real humans. I would also keep one messy checklist of buttons and cards across web and app so I catch when they start drifting into two different products.
