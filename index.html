The exhibit is a walk-through story: one drop leaves rain over Cincinnati, moves through small streams and soil, joins the Ohio River, passes through treatment and habitats and human use, then lifts back into the air as vapor.

I kept asking whether it felt like a route you follow with your feet, not a flat diagram you glance at once.

Role

Concept, UX/UI, visual design, and branding for the full assignment.

Audience

Families, students on field trips, and everyday visitors who are not there to read tiny gray labels.

Goal

Keep the science tied to Ohio so it feels local, not like a generic globe diagram.

Format

Promo website, companion app, and in-building signage mocked into photos.

Why it matters

Plenty of people live near the Ohio River without ever linking that wide band on a map to the water in their tap or the park fountain.

The exhibit tries to keep the story grounded here, in this watershed, instead of floating in abstract planet talk.

Big idea

The water drop is the main character. The floor is broken into named zones on purpose: Precipitation Station, Collection City, Evaporation Enclave, Condensation Country. The names are a little playful so the cycle does not feel like a lecture title list.

Each zone mixes physical pieces with screens, short prompts, and the app so visitors are doing something, not only reading a wall and leaving.

Rainfall → Streams & Soil → Ohio River → Human Life & Ecosystems → Evaporation → Clouds

Final direction

Dark blues, watery gradients, and cyan accents so type and buttons stay readable in uneven gallery light. I also wanted the layout to stay simple enough that families are not decoding the interface while they are trying to learn.

Design priorities

  • Science that invites touching and tapping, not only reading
  • Space for adults and kids to use things at the same time
  • One visual language across signs, screens, and the app
  • Story and visuals that read as Ohio, not stock nature anywhere
  • Clear path through rooms so people do not feel spun around

Type and logo

I tested headline styles until I landed on something warm enough for families but still readable on tall signage and on a small phone screen.

The pairing I kept still reads over busy river photography without feeling stiff, because it had to work on the hero, printed-style signage, and the app without drifting into three unrelated styles.

AI comps

I used AI to move quickly through mood and layout ideas, then deleted most of it. Anything that stayed had to fit the Ohio River narrative, not generic stock scenery.

Early rounds were too literal and textbook-flat. Once prompts named the path, lighting, and materials more clearly, the comps started lining up with how I imagined the exhibit.

I treated AI as early reference, not as finished art I would hand in without redraws or edits.

App on the floor

In the building the app is mostly practical: map, next step, scan codes, show what you already finished, so the walk feels like one connected path instead of random rooms.

Visitor flow I had in mind

  1. 1
    Enter the exhibit

    Open the app, see the journey map, and spot where the walk begins.

  2. 2
    Scan a station marker

    Scan to load the right station and confirm you are in the correct zone.

  3. 3
    Learn and interact

    Short prompts and feedback so the moment feels active instead of reading a long wall label.

  4. 4
    Collect and progress

    Collect stations or badges so the path feels like it is moving forward.

  5. 5
    Complete the journey

    Finish with a clear beat that ties the full water cycle together.

AR scanning

A scan starts a short guided moment tailored to that station.

Collecting stations

Visitors can save zones they find, which gives a reason to keep walking the full route.

Finishing the cycle

The ending loops ecosystems, everyday water use, and evaporation back into a single closing arc.

Learning between taps

Short prompts, feedback, and light progress cues teach the science without slowing families down in a doorway.

On the floor I cared most about two questions: where am I on the map, and what should I do next. If the app answers those, the stations read as one journey instead of separate rooms.

Testing and fixes

I ran a few sessions on ticketing and station pages to see whether people could tell what was interactive.

Ticketing mostly worked, but dense panels made people pause because nothing obvious looked tappable. I responded by strengthening buttons, spacing, and selected states.

Methods

  • Think-aloud while participants tried tickets and clicked through station sections.
  • First-click prompts on busy layouts to see if buttons read as buttons.
  • Short notes after each session, then longer documentation in the report pages below.

Problem

People missed small interactive cues on packed panels.

Change

Stronger prompts, clearer button grouping, light motion only where it helped attention.

Problem

Explore and Stations sections visually blended into the text blocks around them.

Change

More air between chunks, bolder type, and darker panels behind the interactive rows so skimming is faster.

Problem

Ticket time slots did not look locked in when you picked them.

Change

Stronger selected and hover states, and labels that confirm what you just picked.

Moodboard and process

This section is where color, texture, and type started to lock. I wanted a real museum tone that still feels friendly for families.

How I worked through it

  • Inspiration: map apps with readable progress, museum signage with large type, posters that rely on contrast instead of whisper-small text.
  • Iteration: two moodboard directions in parallel before freezing type and color. Direction A felt more energetic for families without turning into visual noise on large panels.
  • Decisions: late edits had to pass a simple check: can a tired visitor read it quickly, and does it still read as one river story end to end.

Direction A felt brighter and more engaging for families, so I moved forward with it.

Direction B was calmer and more refined, but it felt slower for the youngest visitors in the mix.

Reflection

The biggest takeaway for me is how much alignment matters between a physical walkthrough and a phone companion. Brand, web, app, and signage all had to feel like one exhibit, not four separate assignments.

The water cycle was the hardest part because it is easy to land in basic diagram territory. Focusing on one drop moving through Ohio and tying it to real pipes, plants, and faucets is what finally made the story feel alive to me.

Next time I would test ticketing earlier, and I would keep a shared checklist of buttons and cards across web and app so drift between the two is easier to catch.
