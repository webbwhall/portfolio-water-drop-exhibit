Project overview

What this site is

This is my portfolio site for Journey of a Water Drop: The Living Ohio River. It walks through how the exhibit came together, from messy early ideas to the final website and app screens.

The exhibit itself is a museum-style walkthrough. You follow one drop from rain over Cincinnati, through creeks and dirt, into the Ohio River, then into stuff like treatment plants and river life, and back out through evaporation.

I cared a lot about making it feel like a path you walk, not a poster that lists facts in a circle.

Role

I did the exhibit concept, UX/UI, visuals, and branding for the assignment.

Audience

Families with kids, school groups, and general museum visitors who do not live in charts all day.

Goal

Make water science feel close to home in Ohio, not like a random textbook chapter.

Format

Marketing-style website, phone companion app, and signage for inside the building.

Why it matters

A ton of people live near the Ohio River and still never connect the big brown line on a map to the water in their house or park.

I wanted the story to land as something you could picture in Ohio, not a generic planet diagram.

Big idea

I treated the drop like the main character. The floor is split into zones with silly names on purpose: Precipitation Station, Collection City, Evaporation Enclave, Condensation Country.

Each stop mixes real exhibit pieces with screens, short prompts, and the phone app so you are not just reading a wall and walking away.

Rainfall → Streams & Soil → Ohio River → Human Life & Ecosystems → Evaporation → Clouds

Final direction

I went dark and watery on purpose so type and buttons still read under weird gallery light. I also did not want families to need a manual just to figure out where to tap or walk next.

What I wanted the design to do

  • Science that feels like you can touch it, not only read it
  • Room for parents and kids to poke at stuff together
  • Signs, screens, and app that all feel like one place
  • River content that screams Ohio, not any river anywhere
  • Paths you can follow without getting lost between rooms

Type and logo

I tried a bunch of headline styles until I found something friendly for kids but still legible on a tall sign or a small phone frame.

The combo I kept still reads over busy river photos and does not feel stiff. It had to survive the hero, printed signs, and the app without turning into three different brands.

AI comps

I used AI to speed through a bunch of mood and layout ideas, then threw most of it out. What stayed had to fit the Ohio River story, not a random stock photo vibe.

First rounds looked like clip art science class. When I wrote tighter prompts about the museum path and lighting, the images started to match how I pictured the exhibit.

AI was a sketch layer for me, not something I shipped as final art without edits.

App on the floor

In the building I wanted the phone to handle the boring helper stuff: map, next step, scan codes, see what you already finished. If those work, the rooms feel linked instead of random.

How I imagine the visit going

  1. 1
    Walk in

    Open the app, see the map, figure out where the path starts.

  2. 2
    Scan the marker

    Point the camera at the code so the right station loads and you know you are in the correct room.

  3. 3
    Do the station

    Short prompts and little reactions so it feels active, not like reading every word on a wall label.

  4. 4
    Save progress

    Collect stations or badges so you feel the line moving forward.

  5. 5
    Finish the arc

    Last beat loops evaporation and everyday water back into the story so the cycle feels closed.

AR scanning

Scan kicks off a short guided beat for that one station.

Collecting stations

People can save zones they find, which gives them a reason to keep walking the full line.

Finishing the cycle

Ending screen loops wildlife, faucets, and evaporation so the science lands as one story, not loose facts.

Learning between taps

Small prompts and feedback teach the facts without making families stand still in a doorway forever.

On the floor I only really cared about two questions: where am I on this map, and what do I do next. Nail those and the exhibit reads as one path instead of five separate rooms.

Testing and fixes

I ran a few friends and classmates through ticketing and the station pages to see what read as clickable and what did not.

Tickets mostly worked, but people froze on dense panels where nothing looked alive. I leaned harder on obvious buttons, spacing, and selected states after that.

What I actually did

  • Think-aloud while they bought tickets and poked at station pages.
  • First-click tests on the busiest sections to see if buttons looked like buttons.
  • Quick notes after each run, then longer write-ups in the report pages below.

Problem

People missed small interactive cues on packed panels.

Change

Louder prompts, chunkier buttons, tiny motion only where it actually helped someone notice the tap target.

Problem

Explore and Stations sections visually blended into the text blocks around them.

Change

More air between chunks, bolder type, and darker panels behind the interactive rows so skimming is faster.

Problem

Ticket time slots did not look locked in when you picked them.

Change

Stronger selected and hover states, and labels that confirm what you just picked.

Moodboard and process

Where I locked the vibe: still a museum, still fun for kids, still readable when you are half awake on a field trip.

Notes to myself

  • Pulling references: map apps with clear progress, big-type museum signs, posters with real contrast instead of whispery gray type.
  • Trying two looks: I ran Direction A and B side by side before I froze fonts and color. A felt louder and more kid-friendly without turning into chaos on a wall.
  • Cutting late ideas: last-minute stuff only stayed if a tired person could still read it fast and it still felt like the same river story end to end.

Direction A won because it felt more alive for families and matched the energy I wanted on the floor.

Direction B still looks nice on a PDF, but in my head it did not pull little kids through the space the same way.

Reflection

I learned how much work it is to keep a physical walkthrough and a phone app feeling like the same show. Brand, web, app, and signs all had to share one visual language or the whole thing falls apart.

The water cycle was the hardest part because it is easy to make it feel like fifth-grade homework. Once I stuck to one drop moving through Ohio and tied it to real pipes, plants, and faucets, the story finally clicked for me.

If I did it again I would user-test ticketing way earlier. I would also keep one living checklist of buttons and cards across web and app so I notice faster when they start drifting into two different products.
