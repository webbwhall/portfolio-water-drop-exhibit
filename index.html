Project overview

What this site is

This is my portfolio site for Journey of a Water Drop: The Living Ohio River. It walks through how the exhibit came together, from messy early ideas to the final website and app screens.

The exhibit itself is a museum-style walkthrough. You follow one drop from rain over Cincinnati, through creeks and dirt, into the Ohio River, then into stuff like treatment plants and river life, and back out through evaporation.

I wanted it to feel like a path you walk, not a poster that lists facts in a circle.

Role

I did the exhibit concept, UX/UI, visuals, and branding for the assignment.

Audience

Families with kids, school groups, and general museum visitors who do not live in charts all day.

Goal

Make water science feel close to home in Ohio, not like a random textbook chapter.

Format

Marketing-style website, phone companion app, and signage for inside the building.

Why it matters

A ton of people live near the Ohio River and still never connect the big brown line on a map to the water in their house or park.

I wanted the story to land as something you could picture in Ohio, not a generic planet diagram.

Big idea

I treated the drop like the main character. The floor is split into zones with silly names on purpose: Precipitation Station, Collection City, Evaporation Enclave, Condensation Country.

Each stop mixes real exhibit pieces with screens, short prompts, and the phone app so you are not just reading a wall and walking away.

Rainfall → Streams & Soil → Ohio River → Human Life & Ecosystems → Evaporation → Clouds

Final direction

I went dark and watery on purpose so type and buttons still read under weird gallery light. I still wanted it simple enough that families are not fighting the design to understand what to do.

What I wanted the design to do

  • Science that feels like you can touch it, not only read it
  • Room for parents and kids to poke at stuff together
  • Signs, screens, and app that all feel like one place
  • River content that reads clearly as Ohio, not any river anywhere
  • Paths you can follow without getting lost between rooms

I used AI to speed through a bunch of mood and layout ideas, then threw most of it out. What stayed had to fit the Ohio River story, not a random stock photo vibe.

First rounds looked too literal and kind of like a textbook. When I wrote more specific prompts about the museum path and lighting, the images started to match how I pictured the exhibit.

AI was a starting point for me, not something I shipped as final art without edits.

What I focused on

  • Clarity: I tried to make sure every screen had one obvious next tap.
  • Consistency: cards and buttons repeat the same shapes and behaviors so nothing feels like a different app.
  • Momentum: progress and collect moments keep you moving without turning the whole thing into a loud game.

App on the floor

I wanted the app to do the boring helpful stuff in the building: where you are on the map, what to tap at each station, scanning markers, and seeing progress so the path does not feel random.

How I pictured someone using it

  1. 1
    Enter the exhibit

    Open the app and see the journey map and where to start.

  2. 2
    Scan a station marker

    Scan the marker to pull up the station and confirm you are in the right zone.

  3. 3
    Learn and interact

    Quick prompts and feedback help the learning feel active, not like reading a wall label.

  4. 4
    Collect and progress

    Collect stations and badges so the journey feels like it is moving forward.

  5. 5
    Complete the journey

    End with a clear completion moment that ties the whole cycle together.

AR scanning

Scanning a marker kicks off a short guided moment for that station.

Collecting stations

Guests can save zones they find, which nudges them to keep moving through the path.

Finishing the cycle

The ending ties ecosystems, everyday water use, and evaporation back into one arc.

Learning between taps

Short prompts, feedback, and progress cues try to teach the science without slowing people down on the floor.

Mostly I wanted the app to answer two things in the building: where am I on the map, and what should I do next. If it does that, the stations feel connected instead of random rooms.

Testing and fixes

I tested a few key flows to see if people understood what was interactive and if the ticket flow felt clear.

Most people got through the ticket flow, but a few hesitated because it was not always obvious what was interactive. The main changes were making interaction cues clearer and making selected states easier to see.

What I ran

  • I had people talk out loud while they tried ticketing and clicking around stations on the site.
  • On busy panels I asked where they would click first to see if the tappable stuff actually read as tappable.
  • I wrote short notes after each session, then expanded them into the report pages under this section.

Problem

People missed small interactive cues on packed panels.

Change

Bigger prompts, chunkier buttons, tiny motion only where it actually helped.

Problem

Explore and Stations sections visually blended into the text blocks around them.

Change

More air between chunks, bolder type, and darker panels behind the interactive rows so skimming is faster.

Problem

Ticket time slots did not look locked in when you picked them.

Change

Stronger selected and hover states, and labels that confirm what you just picked.

Moodboard and process

This is where I figured out the look and feel. I wanted it to feel like a real museum experience, but still fun and clear for families.

Process notes

  • Inspiration: apps with clear maps and progress, museum signs you can read from far back, and graphics that lean on contrast instead of tiny type.
  • Iteration: I ran two directions in parallel on moodboards before I froze type and color. Direction A won for me because it felt energetic for families without getting messy on big panels.
  • Decision-making: late edits had to pass a simple gut check: can someone tired still read it fast, and does it still feel like one river story the whole way through.

Direction A felt more energetic and more fun for families, so that became the direction I moved forward with.

Direction B felt calmer and more elegant, but it did not feel as engaging for younger visitors.

Reflection

This project helped me understand how physical space and digital design can work together. I had to stay organized so the branding, the website, the app flows, and the signage all felt like the same exhibit.

The hardest part was making the water cycle feel interesting instead of basic. The project got better once I focused on the story of one drop moving through the Ohio River and connecting it to real systems people use every day.

If I did this again I would put the ticket flow in front of people sooner, and I would keep one running list of buttons and cards for both the site and the app so I catch when they start to look like two different products.
