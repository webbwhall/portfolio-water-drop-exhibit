This project is a museum exhibit concept where visitors follow one drop of water from rain in Cincinnati, through streams and soil, into the Ohio River, and then into ecosystems and human systems like water treatment. My goal was to make the water cycle feel like a story you move through, not just a diagram on a wall.

Role

UX/UI Design, Exhibit Concept, Visual Design, Branding

Audience

Families, students, young visitors, museum guests

Goal

Make environmental science interactive, local, and memorable

Format

Website, mobile app, signage, exhibit experience

Why It Matters

The Ohio River affects millions of people across the region, but many visitors never think about where their water comes from or where it goes. This exhibit tries to keep environmental ideas personal and local so they stick with people, instead of staying abstract like a textbook wall panel.

Big Idea

The main idea was to treat water like a character on a journey. Visitors move through stations like Precipitation Station, Collection City, Evaporation Enclave, and Condensation Country. Each zone teaches a part of the cycle using physical elements and digital touchpoints, like screens, interactive prompts, and an app that supports the experience.

Rainfall → Streams & Soil → Ohio River → Human Life & Ecosystems → Evaporation → Clouds

Final Direction

The final direction is dark, water-focused, and high contrast so type and UI read fast on the floor. I kept it simple enough that families can follow it without feeling lost.

Typography & Branding Exploration

I tested different headline styles to find something that feels friendly and educational, but still easy to read on signs and screens.

I went with a style that stays readable over busy backgrounds and still feels approachable. That mattered because this had to work across hero images, signage, and app screens.

AI exploration

AI helped me explore mood and visuals quickly. After that, I narrowed it down so it actually matched the Ohio River story.

The early AI results looked too literal and kind of like a textbook. Once I wrote more specific prompts, the visuals felt more like an exhibit. I treated AI as a starting point, not the final answer.

App as an Interactive Product

The app supports the exhibit by helping visitors know what to do at each station, scan markers, and track progress through the journey.

Usability Testing & Improvements

I tested a few key flows to see if people understood what was interactive and if the ticket flow felt clear.

Most people got through the ticket flow, but a few hesitated because it was not always obvious what was interactive. The main changes were making interaction cues clearer and making selected states easier to see.

What I ran

  • Think-aloud sessions on website ticketing and station browsing tasks.
  • First-click style prompts on dense panels to see if affordances read as tappable.
  • Short debrief notes captured in the report pages below.

Problem

Visitors missed subtle interactive cues on complex panels.

Change

Introduced bolder prompts, segmented buttons, and motion hints where appropriate.

Problem

Explore / Stations visuals blended into informational sections.

Change

Increased type hierarchy, more space, and contrast blocks so scanning the page is easier.

Problem

Ticket time selection states felt tentative.

Change

Strengthened active + hover treatments and paired labels with confirmations.

Moodboard & Creation Process

This is where I figured out the look and feel. I wanted it to feel like a real museum experience, but still fun and clear for families.

Process notes

  • Inspiration: apps with strong map and progress patterns, museum wayfinding that reads at a distance, and environmental graphics that use contrast instead of tiny type.
  • Iteration: two visual directions lived side by side in moodboards before I froze type and color. Direction A won because it matched family energy without feeling chaotic on large panels.
  • Decision-making: every late change went through the same filter: can a tired parent read it in five seconds, and does it still feel like one river story end to end.

Direction A felt more energetic and more fun for families, so that became the direction I moved forward with. Direction B felt calmer and more elegant, but it did not feel as engaging for younger visitors.

Reflection

This project helped me understand how physical space and digital design can work together. I had to stay organized so the branding, the website, the app flows, and the signage all felt like the same exhibit.

The hardest part was making the water cycle feel interesting instead of basic. The project got better once I focused on the story of one drop moving through the Ohio River and connecting it to real systems people use every day.

If I repeated the sequence, I would start usability checks earlier on the ticket flow and keep one shared component checklist between web and app so drift shows up sooner.
